What it is

A browser visualization that runs Bach’s Fugue No. 2 in C minor, BWV 847 from The Well-Tempered Clavier, Book I (1722), through a composer and a decomposer side by side, in real time, on the same audio timeline.

The screen is split into two panels. The left panel is the forward pass — composition → decomposition: the fugue subject enters as a single melodic line, then voice by voice the demo unfolds the full polyphonic texture (subject entries, inversions, augmentations, stretto). You watch the encoder build the piece up. The right panel is the backward pass — decomposition → reconstruction: the full polyphony enters, then the demo decomposes it back into per-voice projections and lines them up with the original subject. You watch the decoder take the piece apart.

Both passes run on the same audio timeline. The same Bach plays. The two panels move in lock-step. The point is to make the symmetry visible: the operators that build the fugue and the operators that take it apart are mirror images of each other.

Live demo →

The whole demo is a single self-contained HTML file with no build step, no server, and no external dependencies beyond a modern browser. Audio is opt-in.

Why it matters — bio digital twins

A bio digital twin is a computational model of a specific biological system — a section of cortex, a hepatic lobule, a heart chamber — wired tightly enough that it can predict the system’s response to a stimulus. Building a twin that predicts (forward pass) is one half of the work. Building a twin that retrodicts (backward pass) — that answers what stimulus produced this measured state? — is the other half. The two are intimately related, and most of the practical value of a digital twin lives in being able to do both.

The pipeline visualization makes the forward/backward symmetry mechanically obvious on a small enough example to fit in a browser tab. The fugue is a deliberate choice: it is one of the cleanest documented examples in the wild of a complex object that was built by composition and is fully decomposable back into its parts. Bach’s contrapuntal rules guarantee both directions.

The framework’s interest in this is structural. If you have an aggregate that was assembled from many partial views via per-view return operators, when can you go back the other way and recover the per-view contributions? The fugue is a concrete answer in one specific case (yes, fully, and you can hear it). The general framework question is much harder, and the v0.1 draft handles it conditionally — see What is not in the paper.

The framing for the trio (generator + cell simulator + scanner) is the trio white paper. This pipeline visualization is not part of the trio — it is a paired companion to the decoder-of-the-encoder repository, both using BWV 847 as their canonical example.

How it works — two panels, one timeline

The fugue subject is the musical equivalent of a function f. Each voice i of the fugue applies a projection Πi to the subject — a transposition, an inversion, an augmentation in duration, a delay in entry — producing the voice’s local rendition. The complete fugue is the aggregate of all voices, with contrapuntal rules controlling how they interact.

The forward pass shown in the left panel walks this composition step by step:

subject → Π_1(subject) → + Π_2(subject) → + Π_3(subject) → ... → full fugue

Each step adds one voice’s transformed copy onto what is already playing. By the end of the pass, the full polyphonic texture is on the screen and in the audio.

The backward pass shown in the right panel walks the inverse:

full fugue → identify voice 1, subtract Π_1(subject) → identify voice 2, subtract Π_2(subject) → ... → bare subject

Each step removes one voice’s contribution and reveals what is left. By the end of the pass, only the bare subject remains, and the per-voice transformations have been recovered as separate panels alongside.

Both passes share the same time index. As Bach’s audio plays, the corresponding step in each pass advances. The left panel’s “we are now adding voice 3” lights up at the same wall-clock moment as the right panel’s “we have now identified and subtracted voice 3.”

The musical example is what makes this work. A fugue is constructively decomposable by definition — the contrapuntal rules that built it are also the rules that take it apart. The pipeline visualization makes that constructive symmetry visible step by step.

Reading the visualization

When you open the live demo:

  • The audio begins playing (opt-in mute is available).
  • Two panels appear, left for forward, right for backward.
  • The fugue subject appears in both panels as the starting point.
  • As the audio advances, both panels animate: the left panel adds voices, the right panel subtracts them.
  • The two passes meet at the middle of the timeline — the moment when the full polyphony is on screen and the decomposition has just begun.
  • By the end, the right panel has resolved back to the bare subject, plus a per-voice diagram showing each transformation that was applied.

What to look for:

  • Symmetry of timing. The forward pass adds voice k at the same moment the backward pass removes voice k. The mirror reflection is exact.
  • Recovery of transformations. As each voice is decomposed out, the right panel shows what kind of transformation that voice applied — inversion, augmentation, transposition, delay. The user can read these off the diagram.
  • Constructive symmetry. The two passes use the same operators. There is no “trained inverse network” doing the decomposition. The contrapuntal rules of the fugue are themselves the inverse rules.

The companion — drt-decoder-of-the-encoder

The companion repository decoder-of-the-encoder takes the same fugue and lays it out as a navigable tree — voice by voice, subject entry by subject entry, note by note. Where the pipeline visualization makes the forward/backward symmetry visible as a temporal flow, the decoder visualization makes the static structural decomposition navigable as a spatial graph.

The two are deliberately released as a pair. They share the musical example, the conceptual framework, and the rendering aesthetic. They illustrate two different aspects of the same compose-decompose question:

  • Pipeline shows you that the forward and backward operators are mirror images on the same timeline.
  • Decoder lets you walk the static decomposition by hand and see the full structure at once.

Together they cover both the dynamic and static views of the same object.

What is not in the paper

The honest list of what this paper deliberately does not claim:

  • A general inversion theorem. The pipeline shows that the fugue is constructively invertible because Bach’s rules guarantee it. That does not generalize to arbitrary aggregates of partial observers. The framework’s general statements about decomposability are conditional on hypotheses spelled out in the v0.1 draft.
  • A norm-growth claim. Earlier prose around this family of demos asserted that the aggregate of many partial views had bigger norm than the original signal — that ∥R(f)∥ > ∥f∥. That inequality has been retracted in the v0.1 draft. The current claim is conditional and weaker: under four hypotheses and a bounded computational budget, the aggregate exposes structural features that are not accessible from the original signal alone within the same budget. The pipeline is an artistic example of forward/backward symmetry, not a proof of the conditional claim.
  • A general decoder for noisy aggregates. The pipeline runs on a clean fugue with no noise, no missing voices, no incomplete timing. Real bio digital twin inverse problems have all three. Solving the inverse problem in the noisy case requires additional machinery (regularisation, posterior sampling, Bayesian inverse methods) that the pipeline does not provide.
  • An original musicology contribution. The decomposition is faithful to the score and to standard fugue analysis. Music theorists have analyzed BWV 847 for three centuries. The visualization makes the analysis interactive; it does not extend it.
